Essays

There's something magical about the moment when a student finally grasps why we say "went" instead of "goed." It's not just about memorizing exceptions—it's about understanding that language is alive, shaped by centuries of human creativity and accident. These irregularities aren't bugs in the system; they're features that tell the story of how we've lived and spoken together across time.
Every language has words that resist translation—concepts so culturally specific that they can only be approximated in other tongues. The German "Fernweh," the Portuguese "saudade," the Japanese "komorebi." These words don't just describe experiences; they create them. They give us permission to feel things we might not have noticed otherwise, to pay attention to nuances of human experience that might slip by unnamed.
When my students switch between languages mid-sentence, they're not making mistakes. They're making choices. Each language carries its own emotional register, its own way of being in the world. Code-switching isn't just about communication—it's about inhabiting different versions of yourself, each authentic, each necessary.
